1. Introduction & Units Ch 1

Scientific Notation & Metric Prefixes UnitsPractice ▼
☆ ○
Powers of Ten: 106 = 1,000,000 (Mega, M)
103 = 1,000 (kilo, k) · 10-3 = 0.001 (milli, m)
10-6 = 0.000001 (micro, μ) · 10-9 = 0.000000001 (nano, n)
10-12 = 0.000000000001 (pico, p)

Practice Problems:

1. Convert 0.0047 to scientific notation.

2. Express 47,000 Ω in kΩ and MΩ.

3. Convert 3.5 × 106 to standard form.

4. A current is 2.5 mA. Express in amperes.

Answers

1. 0.0047 = 4.7 × 10-3

2. 47,000 Ω = 47 kΩ = 0.047 MΩ

3. 3.5 × 106 = 3,500,000

4. 2.5 mA = 2.5 × 10-3 A = 0.0025 A

Significant Figures & Rounding AccuracyPractice ▼
☆ ○
Non-zero digits are always significant. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
Trailing zeros after decimal are significant. Leading zeros are not.

Practice: How many significant figures in: (a) 3.14 (b) 0.0025 (c) 100.0 (d) 500

Answers

(a) 3.14 → 3 sig figs

(b) 0.0025 → 2 sig figs

(c) 100.0 → 4 sig figs

(d) 500 → 1 sig fig (ambiguous without decimal)

2. Voltage & Current Ch 2

Voltage: V = W / Q FormulaPractice ▼
☆ ○
V = W / Q where V = voltage (V), W = energy (J), Q = charge (C).
1 volt = 1 joule per coulomb.

Practice Problems:

1. 60 J of energy moves 15 C of charge. Find voltage.

2. How much energy is needed to move 20 C through 12 V?

Solutions

1. V = W / Q = 60 / 15 = 4 V

2. W = V × Q = 12 × 20 = 240 J

Current: I = Q / t FormulaPractice ▼
☆ ○
I = Q / t where I = current (A), Q = charge (C), t = time (s).
Q = n × e where e = 1.602 × 10-19 C.

Practice Problems:

1. 30 C passes through a wire in 15 s. Find current.

2. How many electrons pass through a point in 2 s if I = 3 A?

3. A current of 5 A flows for 10 minutes. What charge has passed?

Solutions

1. I = 30 / 15 = 2 A

2. Q = 3 × 2 = 6 C. n = Q/e = 6 / (1.602×10-19) = 3.745 × 1019 electrons

3. t = 10 min = 600 s. Q = 5 × 600 = 3000 C

Battery & Ampere-Hour Rating TheoryPractice ▼
☆ ○
Ampere-hour (Ah) rating indicates the total charge a battery can deliver.
100 Ah can supply 1 A for 100 hours or 10 A for 10 hours. Ah = I × t.

Practice Problems:

1. A 50 Ah battery powers a device drawing 2.5 A. How long?

2. A device draws 0.5 A. How many hours from an 80 Ah battery?

Solutions

1. t = Ah / I = 50 / 2.5 = 20 hours

2. t = 80 / 0.5 = 160 hours

Conductors, Insulators, Semiconductors Theory ▼
☆ ○
Free electron: An electron that has broken free from its parent atom and moves freely through the metal lattice. These form the "electron gas" that conducts current when an electric field is applied.
Conductor (Cu, Al, Ag): Free electrons in outer shell → current flows easily. Low resistivity.
Insulator (rubber, glass, plastic): Tightly bound electrons → very high resistance.
Semiconductor (Si, Ge): Conductivity between conductor and insulator; controllable via doping (adding impurities).
Active & Passive Elements Theory ▼
☆ ○
Active elements supply energy to a circuit: battery, generator, transistor, op-amp.
Passive elements consume, store, or dissipate energy: resistor, capacitor, inductor.
Oscillatory (RLC) circuit: A resistor, inductor, and capacitor in series/parallel form a damped oscillator. The resistor dissipates energy as heat, causing oscillations to decay. Mechanical analog: mass-spring-damper system. Mass ↔ inductor (stores kinetic/current energy), spring ↔ capacitor (stores potential/voltage energy), damper ↔ resistor (dissipates as heat/friction).

3. Resistance Ch 3

Resistance: R = ρL / A FormulaResistivity ▼
☆ ○
R = ρ L / A where R = resistance (Ω), ρ = resistivity (Ω·m), L = length (m), A = area (m2).
Resistance (R) is a property of a specific object (depends on size/shape). Resistivity (ρ) is an intrinsic material property. A long thin wire has more R than a short thick wire of the same material.
Temperature effect: R2 = R1[1 + α(T2 - T1)].
For metals: α > 0 (R increases with temperature). For semiconductors: α < 0 (R decreases with temperature).

Practice: Copper wire (ρ = 1.72×10-8 Ω·m), L = 50 m, A = 2×10-6 m2. Find R. If the length is doubled, what is the new R?

Solution

Step 1: R = (1.72×10-8 × 50) / (2×10-6)

Step 2: R = 8.6×10-7 / 2×10-6 = 0.43 Ω

Step 3: If L doubles to 100 m: R = 0.43 × 2 = 0.86 Ω

Resistor Color Code TheoryPractice ▼
☆ ○
Color Code Chart:
Black 0 · Brown 1 · Red 2 · Orange 3 · Yellow 4 · Green 5 · Blue 6 · Violet 7 · Gray 8 · White 9
Band 1 & 2: digits. Band 3: multiplier (power of 10). Band 4: tolerance (gold = ±5%, silver = ±10%).

Practice Problems:

1. Red, Violet, Orange, Gold → nominal R and tolerance?

2. Brown, Black, Red, Silver → nominal R?

3. A 4.7 kΩ resistor with ±5% tolerance: what are the band colors?

Solutions

1. Red=2, Violet=7, Orange=×103, Gold=±5% → 27 kΩ ±5%

2. Brown=1, Black=0, Red=×102, Silver=±10% → 1 kΩ ±10%

3. 4.7 kΩ = 47 × 102 → Yellow (4), Violet (7), Red (×102), Gold (±5%)

Thermistors, LDRs & Temperature Effects TheoryDevices ▼
☆ ○
Thermistor: Temperature-sensitive resistor. NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient): R decreases as temperature increases. PTC (Positive Temperature Coefficient): R increases as temperature increases. The negative slope on an I-V curve means current increases faster than voltage (R is decreasing).
LDR (Photoconductive cell): Light Dependent Resistor. R decreases when exposed to light. More light → more charge carriers → lower R. Used in automatic light sensors, camera exposure meters.
Varistor (VDR): Voltage-dependent resistor. R decreases as voltage increases. Used for surge protection.
Temperature coefficient: R2 = R1[1 + α(T2 - T1)]. α in °C-1.

Practice: A copper wire has R = 10 Ω at 20°C. α = 0.00393 /°C. Find R at 80°C.

Solution

Step 1: R2 = 10 [1 + 0.00393(80 - 20)]

Step 2: R2 = 10 [1 + 0.00393 × 60]

Step 3: R2 = 10 [1 + 0.2358] = 10 × 1.2358 = 12.36 Ω

4. Ohm's Law, Power & Energy Ch 4

Ohm's Law: V = IR FormulaPractice ▼
☆ ○
V = IR · I = V/R · R = V/I
Scalar form: V = IR.   Vector form: J = σE (J = current density A/m2, σ = conductivity S/m, E = electric field V/m).

Practice:

1. A 12 V battery across a 4 Ω resistor. Find current.

2. A current of 2 A flows through an 8 Ω resistor. Find voltage drop.

3. A 9 V source produces 3 A. Find resistance.

Solutions

1. I = V/R = 12/4 = 3 A

2. V = IR = 2 × 8 = 16 V

3. R = V/I = 9/3 = 3 Ω

Power & Energy FormulaPractice ▼
☆ ○
P = V × I = I2R = V2/R (Watts)
Energy: W = P × t (Joules). kWh = kW × h.
Efficiency: η = (Pout / Pin) × 100%

Practice:

1. A 60 W bulb on 120 V. Find current and resistance.

2. A heater draws 10 A from 240 V. Find power and energy used in 3 hours (kWh).

3. A motor draws 500 W, delivers 400 W. Find efficiency.

Solutions

1. I = 60/120 = 0.5 A. R = 1202/60 = 240 Ω

2. P = 240 × 10 = 2400 W. Energy = 2.4 kW × 3 h = 7.2 kWh

3. η = (400/500) × 100% = 80%

Fuses, Circuit Breakers & GFCIs Theory ▼
☆ ○
Fuse: A thin wire that melts when current exceeds its rating. One-time use (must be replaced). Fast-acting.
Circuit Breaker: A bimetallic strip (heat) or electromagnet (instant) that trips a switch. Reusable (just reset). Common in household panels.
GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter): Detects an imbalance (>5 mA) between hot and neutral wires. If current leaks through a person to ground, the GFCI cuts power in milliseconds. Required in bathrooms, kitchens, outdoors. Prevents electrocution.

5. Series DC Circuits Ch 5

Series Circuits & KVL FormulaKVL ▼
☆ ○
RT = R1 + R2 + R3 + ... · Same current flows through all series resistors.
KVL (Kirchhoff's Voltage Law): The sum of voltage drops around any closed loop equals the applied voltage. ΣVdrops = Vsource.

Practice: R1=1kΩ, R2=2kΩ, R3=3kΩ in series with 12V.

(i) RT (ii) Is (iii) V1, V2, V3 (iv) Verify KVL.

Solution

Step 1: RT = 1k + 2k + 3k = 6 kΩ

Step 2: Is = 12/6000 = 2 mA

Step 3: V1 = 2mA×1k = 2 V, V2 = 2mA×2k = 4 V, V3 = 2mA×3k = 6 V

Step 4 (KVL check): 2 + 4 + 6 = 12 V ✓

Voltage Divider Rule (VDR) FormulaPractice ▼
☆ ○
Vx = (Rx / RT) × Vs
The voltage across any resistor in a series circuit equals its resistance fraction of the total.
Derivation: I = Vs/RT. Then Vx = I × Rx = (Vs/RT) × Rx = Vs × (Rx/RT).

Practice:

1. R1=10Ω, R2=30Ω in series with 40V. Find V1, V2 using VDR.

2. R1=1kΩ, R2=2kΩ, R3=1kΩ, 24V. Find V2.

Solutions

1. RT = 40Ω. V1 = (10/40)×40 = 10 V. V2 = (30/40)×40 = 30 V

2. RT = 1+2+1 = 4kΩ. V2 = (2k/4k)×24 = 12 V

Power in Series Circuits Practice ▼
☆ ○
Ps = Vs × Is (power supplied by source).
PR = I2R (power dissipated by resistor).
Conservation: ΣPR = Ps

Practice: R1=1Ω, R2=2Ω, R3=3Ω in series with 12V.

(i) Ps (ii) P1, P2, P3 (iii) Verify ΣP = Ps

Solution

Step 1: RT = 6Ω, I = 12/6 = 2A

Step 2: Ps = 12 × 2 = 24 W

Step 3: P1 = 22×1 = 4W, P2 = 22×2 = 8W, P3 = 22×3 = 12W

Step 4: 4 + 8 + 12 = 24W = Ps ✓

6. Parallel DC Circuits Ch 6

Parallel Circuits & KCL FormulaKCL ▼
☆ ○
1/RT = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + ...
For 2 resistors: RT = (R1R2)/(R1+R2)
For n equal R: RT = R / n
KCL (Kirchhoff's Current Law): IT = I1 + I2 + ... (current entering = sum leaving)

Practice: R1=6Ω, R2=3Ω, R3=2Ω in parallel with 12V.

(i) RT (ii) IT (iii) I1, I2, I3 (iv) Verify KCL.

Solution

Step 1: 1/RT = 1/6 + 1/3 + 1/2 = 1/6 + 2/6 + 3/6 = 1 → RT = 1 Ω

Step 2: IT = 12/1 = 12 A

Step 3: I1 = 12/6 = 2 A, I2 = 12/3 = 4 A, I3 = 12/2 = 6 A

Step 4 (KCL check): 2 + 4 + 6 = 12 A ✓

Current Divider Rule (CDR) FormulaPractice ▼
☆ ○
Ix = (RT / Rx) × IT
For 2 resistors: I1 = (R2 / (R1+R2)) × IT
I2 = (R1 / (R1+R2)) × IT
More current flows through the smaller resistor.

Practice:

1. R1=6Ω, R2=3Ω parallel, IT=3A. Find I1, I2.

2. R1=12Ω, R2=4Ω parallel, IT=8A. Find I1, I2.

Solutions

1. I1 = (3/(6+3))×3 = 1 A. I2 = (6/(6+3))×3 = 2 A

2. I1 = (4/(12+4))×8 = (4/16)×8 = 2 A. I2 = (12/16)×8 = 6 A

Open & Short Circuits in Parallel Theory ▼
☆ ○
Open circuit: R = ∞, I = 0, V = full voltage across the open. Other branches unaffected.
Short circuit: R = 0, I = maximum (limited only by source), V = 0 across the short. Current bypasses all other parallel branches. Very large total current → blown fuse/tripped breaker.

7. Series-Parallel Networks Ch 7

Reduce & Return Approach MethodPractice ▼
☆ ○
Reduce: Combine series and parallel resistors step-by-step from the farthest point from the source, reducing the network to a single equivalent RT. Find total current Is = Vs/RT.
Return: Work backwards, using VDR and CDR to find voltages and currents in each original resistor.

Practice: R1=10Ω (series with source), R2=20Ω and R3=30Ω in parallel. Source = 12V. Find all currents.

Solution

Step 1 (Reduce): R2||R3 = (20×30)/(20+30) = 12 Ω

Step 2: RT = 10 + 12 = 22 Ω

Step 3: Is = 12/22 = 0.545 A

Step 4 (Return): V1 = 0.545 × 10 = 5.45 V

Step 5: Vparallel = 12 - 5.45 = 6.55 V

Step 6: I2 = 6.55/20 = 0.328 A, I3 = 6.55/30 = 0.218 A

Common Mistake

Mistake: Stopping after finding RT and Is. You must "return" to find each resistor's specific values.

Loaded Voltage Divider ConceptPractice ▼
☆ ○
When a load resistor RL is connected across the output of a voltage divider, the effective resistance changes, altering the output voltage. This is called loading. The output voltage drops compared to the unloaded case.

Practice: R1=R2=10kΩ, Vs=20V. A 10kΩ load is connected across R2. Find loaded and unloaded Vout.

Solution

Step 1 (Unloaded): Vout = (10k/(10k+10k))×20 = 10 V

Step 2 (Loaded): R2||RL = (10×10)/(10+10) = 5 kΩ

Step 3: Vout = (5k/(10k+5k))×20 = 6.67 V

Ladder Networks Practice ▼
☆ ○
Method: Start from the farthest end, combine series and parallel step-by-step toward the source. Then return from source to find branch currents/voltages.

Practice: R1=2Ω (series from source), then node with R3=2Ω to ground, then R2=4Ω (series), then R4=4Ω to ground. Vs=20V. Find RT.

Solution

Step 1: Start from end: R4 = 4 Ω to ground

Step 2: R2 + R4 = 4 + 4 = 8 Ω

Step 3: R3 || (R2+R4) = 2 || 8 = (2×8)/(10) = 1.6 Ω

Step 4: RT = R1 + 1.6 = 2 + 1.6 = 3.6 Ω

8. Methods of Analysis Ch 8

Source Conversion MethodPractice ▼
☆ ○
Voltage → Current: I = V/R, resistor R in parallel.
Current → Voltage: V = I × R, resistor R in series.

Practice:

1. Convert 12V in series with 6Ω to a current source.

2. Convert 3A in parallel with 4Ω to a voltage source.

Solutions

1. I = 12/6 = 2 A in parallel with 6 Ω

2. V = 3 × 4 = 12 V in series with 4 Ω

Mesh Analysis MethodPractice ▼
☆ ○
Steps: (1) Assign clockwise mesh currents for each loop. (2) Apply KVL to each mesh. (3) For resistors shared between meshes, voltage = (I1 - I2)R. (4) Solve the system of equations.

Practice: V1=10V in mesh 1, R1=2Ω, shared R2=4Ω, mesh 2 has R3=2Ω, V2=5V (opposite polarity). Find mesh currents.

Solution

Step 1 (Mesh 1): 10 = 2I1 + 4(I1-I2) → 10 = 6I1 - 4I2

Step 2 (Mesh 2): -5 = 2I2 + 4(I2-I1) → -5 = -4I1 + 6I2

Step 3: Multiply eq1 by 3: 30 = 18I1 - 12I2. Multiply eq2 by 2: -10 = -8I1 + 12I2

Step 4 (Add): 20 = 10I1 → I1 = 2 A

Step 5: 10 = 6(2) - 4I2 → 10 = 12 - 4I2 → I2 = 0.5 A

Nodal Analysis MethodPractice ▼
☆ ○
Steps: (1) Select reference node (ground). (2) Assign voltages V1, V2 at remaining nodes. (3) Apply KCL at each node: sum of currents leaving = 0. (4) Use I = V/R. (5) Solve.

Practice: Node 1: R1=2Ω to ground, R2=4Ω to Node 2. Node 2: R3=2Ω to ground. Current source 3A into Node 1. Find V1, V2.

Solution

Step 1 (Node 1): 3 = V1/2 + (V1-V2)/4 → 12 = 3V1 - V2

Step 2 (Node 2): 0 = V2/2 + (V2-V1)/4 → 0 = 3V2 - V1 → V1 = 3V2

Step 3: 12 = 3(3V2) - V2 = 8V2 → V2 = 1.5 V

Step 4: V1 = 3 × 1.5 = 4.5 V

Wye (Y) ↔ Delta (Δ) Conversion FormulaPractice ▼
☆ ○
Δ → Y: R1 = (RaRb) / (Ra+Rb+Rc)
R2 = (RbRc) / (sum) · R3 = (RaRc) / (sum)
Y → Δ: Ra = (R1R2+R2R3+R3R1) / R1
Rb = (sum) / R2 · Rc = (sum) / R3

Practice: Convert Δ: Ra=10Ω, Rb=20Ω, Rc=30Ω to Y.

Solution

Step 1: Sum = 10 + 20 + 30 = 60

Step 2: R1 = (10×20)/60 = 200/60 = 3.33 Ω

Step 3: R2 = (20×30)/60 = 600/60 = 10 Ω

Step 4: R3 = (10×30)/60 = 300/60 = 5 Ω

Smoke Detector & Bridge Circuit Application ▼
☆ ○
Ionization smoke detectors use a Wheatstone bridge circuit. A small radioactive source (americium-241) ionizes air between two plates, creating a small current (one arm of the bridge). Smoke particles entering the chamber disrupt this current, changing the resistance of that arm. The bridge becomes unbalanced, producing a voltage difference that triggers the alarm circuit.

9. Network Theorems Ch 9

Exam scope: only Superposition.

Superposition Theorem TheoremPractice ▼
☆ ○
Statement: In any linear circuit with multiple independent sources, the current through or voltage across any element is the algebraic sum of the contributions from each source acting alone. When one source is active, all other independent sources are deactivated: voltage sources → short circuit, current sources → open circuit.

Practice: V1=20V (left loop), I2=2A (current source in right branch). R1=10Ω (left), R2=20Ω (shared), R3=10Ω (right). Find current through R2 using superposition.

Solution

Step 1 (V1 alone, I2 open): RT = 10 + 20 = 30 Ω

Step 2: IR2 = 20/30 = 0.667 A (downward)

Step 3 (I2 alone, V1 short): Using CDR: IR2 = (10/(10+20)) × 2 = 0.667 A (upward)

Step 4 (Superpose): IR2 = 0.667 - 0.667 = 0 A

Common Mistake

Mistake: Forgetting to account for direction (sign) when summing contributions. Always mark reference directions.

Q2 - Voltage Divider Rule & Thermistor ExamTheory+Maths ▼

(c) RT=6Ω, Is=15/6=2.5A. V1=2.5V, V2=5V, V3=7.5V. Ps=37.5W, P1=6.25W, P2=12.5W, P3=18.75W. Sum=37.5W ✓

Q4 - Superposition Theorem ExamPractice ▼
☆ ○

(a) State and explain Superposition theorem.

(b) Apply to circuit with voltage source + current source.

(c) Using superposition, find current through the 12Ω resistor (Fig.3). (Assume V=24V, I=2A, R1=6Ω, R2=12Ω, R3=6Ω)

Solution

(a) Each source contributes independently; sum contributions algebraically. Deactivate: V→short, I→open.

(b) (1) V alone: open I. (2) I alone: short V. (3) Add with signs.

(c) V alone: IR2 = 24/(6+12) = 1.333 A (right)

I alone: IR2 = (6/(6+12))×2 = 0.667 A (left)

Net: 1.333 - 0.667 = 0.667 A (right)

↑
